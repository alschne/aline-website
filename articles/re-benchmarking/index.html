How Often Should Companies Re-Benchmark Salaries?

If you’re wondering how often to re-benchmark salaries, you’re already ahead of most companies.

Compensation isn’t something you set once and forget. Markets move. Expectations shift. And if your salary data is outdated, you’re making decisions based on a reality that no longer exists.

So how often should companies re-benchmark salaries?

The Short Answer

Most companies should re-benchmark salaries at least once per year.

But that’s the baseline—not the full story.

The right frequency depends on how fast your company is growing, how competitive your talent market is, and how critical compensation is to your hiring and retention strategy.

Why Salary Benchmarking Needs to Be Ongoing

Salary benchmarking is about aligning your pay with the external market. The problem is: the market doesn’t stand still.

In the last few years alone, compensation has shifted rapidly due to:

  • Increased competition for talent
  • Remote work expanding hiring markets
  • Industry-specific salary inflation
  • Changing expectations around total compensation

If you’re using data that’s even 12–18 months old, you may already be off.

And when compensation is off, it shows up quickly:

  • Offers get declined
  • Employees leave for higher pay
  • Internal pay gaps widen
  • Hiring timelines get longer

A More Realistic Benchmarking Schedule

Instead of thinking in rigid timelines, it’s more useful to think in triggers.

1. Annual Benchmarking (Minimum Standard)

At a minimum, companies should review and update salary benchmarks once per year, typically aligned with:

  • Annual compensation reviews
  • Budget planning cycles
  • Performance review periods

This ensures your salary bands don’t drift too far from market reality.

2. Benchmark When You’re Hiring

If you’re actively hiring—especially for new or hard-to-fill roles—you should validate compensation before opening the role.

Relying on old salary bands during hiring is one of the fastest ways to:

  • Lose strong candidates
  • Waste time on misaligned expectations
  • Damage your employer brand

3. Benchmark During Periods of Growth or Change

You should revisit compensation more frequently if your company is:

  • Scaling quickly
  • Entering new markets or geographies
  • Hiring for new types of roles
  • Competing with larger or better-funded companies

Growth amplifies compensation mistakes. What worked for a 20-person company often breaks at 100.

4. Benchmark When You’re Losing Talent

If employees are leaving and compensation is even a possible factor, don’t wait for your annual cycle.

Exit data is one of the clearest signals that your pay may be out of sync with the market.

What “Re-Benchmarking” Actually Means

Re-benchmarking isn’t just pulling a few salary numbers from a tool and calling it done.

A proper benchmarking process includes:

  • Reviewing fresh, high-quality market data
  • Matching roles accurately (not just titles)
  • Adjusting for geography, industry, and company stage
  • Re-evaluating salary bands—not just individual salaries
  • Checking internal equity alongside external competitiveness

Skipping these steps is how companies end up with “data-driven” decisions that are still wrong.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using Outdated Data

If you’re relying on last year’s data (or older), you’re likely underestimating current market rates.

Benchmarking Only When There’s a Problem

By the time you’re reacting to declined offers or resignations, you’re already behind.

Treating Benchmarking as a One-Time Project

Compensation is a system, not a task. It needs maintenance.

Ignoring Internal Alignment

External competitiveness doesn’t matter if your internal pay structure is inconsistent or unfair.

A Practical Rule of Thumb

If a client asked for a simple rule, it would be this:

Re-benchmark salaries annually—and more often if you’re hiring, growing, or seeing signs of misalignment.

If you’re unsure whether it’s time, it probably is.

Final Thought

Compensation doesn’t fix itself over time. It drifts.

The companies that stay competitive aren’t the ones with the most data—they’re the ones that revisit it regularly and use it thoughtfully.

If you treat salary benchmarking as an ongoing process instead of a periodic task, you’ll make better decisions, hire more effectively, and retain the people you’ve worked hard to bring in.
