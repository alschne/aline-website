# The Biggest Mistakes Companies Make When Benchmarking Pay (And How to Avoid Them)

Compensation benchmarking is supposed to bring clarity. In reality, it often creates confusion—and worse, costly mistakes.

Companies rely on salary data to stay competitive, retain talent, and make fair offers. But when benchmarking is done incorrectly, it can lead to underpaying employees, losing top performers, or overspending without realizing it.

Here are the most common mistakes companies make when benchmarking pay—and what to do instead.

---

## 1. Comparing the Wrong Job Levels

One of the most common—and most damaging—mistakes is mismatching job levels.

A “Marketing Manager” at one company may function like a senior individual contributor, while at another, they may lead a team and own strategy. Titles are not standardized, and benchmarking based on title alone creates misleading comparisons.

**Why it matters:**  
If you benchmark against the wrong level, your salary ranges will be off—either too high or too low—leading to internal inequity or offer declines.

**What to do instead:**  
Focus on **scope, responsibilities, and impact**, not titles. Ask:
- Does the role manage people?
- What level of decision-making authority does it have?
- How complex is the work?

Accurate leveling is the foundation of accurate benchmarking.

---

## 2. Ignoring Geography (or Oversimplifying It)

Location still plays a major role in compensation—even in remote environments.

Many companies either:
- Ignore geography entirely, or  
- Apply overly simplistic adjustments (like “+10% for major cities”)

Both approaches miss the nuance of real market data.

**Why it matters:**  
Paying a “national average” can make you uncompetitive in high-cost markets—and overpay in lower-cost ones.

**What to do instead:**  
Use **location-specific data** whenever possible. If your workforce is remote, define a clear compensation philosophy:
- Location-based pay  
- National bands  
- Tiered geographic zones  

Then apply it consistently.

---

## 3. Relying on Outdated Salary Data

Salary data has a shelf life—and it’s shorter than most companies think.

Markets shift quickly due to:
- Economic changes  
- Talent shortages  
- Industry demand  

Using data that’s even 12–24 months old can significantly distort your benchmarks.

**Why it matters:**  
Outdated data leads to offers that feel out of touch, causing candidate drop-off and retention issues.

**What to do instead:**  
Use **recent, high-quality data sources** and refresh your benchmarks regularly. Ideally:
- Review critical roles at least annually  
- Re-check fast-moving roles (like tech or sales) more frequently  

---

## 4. Treating Salary Surveys as Absolute Truth

Salary surveys are useful—but they are not perfect.

They often vary in:
- Methodology  
- Sample size  
- Data quality  

Blindly trusting a single survey can give a false sense of accuracy.

**Why it matters:**  
Over-reliance on one source increases the risk of skewed data influencing major compensation decisions.

**What to do instead:**  
Use **multiple data sources** and apply judgment. Look for patterns across datasets rather than relying on a single number.

Benchmarking is both art and science.

---

## 5. Benchmarking Once and Never Updating

Compensation benchmarking is not a one-time project.

Yet many companies treat it as a “set it and forget it” exercise—only revisiting when there’s a problem.

**Why it matters:**  
Markets evolve. Roles evolve. Your company evolves. Static benchmarks quickly become irrelevant.

**What to do instead:**  
Build benchmarking into an ongoing process:
- Annual compensation reviews  
- Regular market checks  
- Continuous adjustments based on hiring and retention data  

---

## 6. Ignoring Internal Equity

External benchmarking is only half the picture.

Companies sometimes focus so heavily on market data that they forget to check how pay compares internally.

**Why it matters:**  
Even if your salaries are competitive externally, internal inconsistencies can damage morale and trust.

**What to do instead:**  
Balance **external competitiveness** with **internal equity**:
- Audit pay across similar roles  
- Identify compression issues  
- Ensure consistent leveling and pay ranges  

---

## 7. Not Defining a Compensation Strategy First

Benchmarking without a strategy is like using a map without a destination.

Are you trying to:
- Lead the market?
- Match the market?
- Lag the market with other benefits?

Without a clear philosophy, benchmarking results can lead to inconsistent decisions.

**What to do instead:**  
Define your compensation strategy upfront:
- Target percentile (e.g., 50th, 75th)
- Approach to bonuses, equity, and benefits
- How you differentiate top performers  

Then benchmark against that strategy—not in a vacuum.

---

## Final Thoughts

Compensation benchmarking is a powerful tool—but only when used correctly.

The biggest mistakes companies make—misaligned job levels, outdated data, poor geographic adjustments, and one-time benchmarking—can quietly undermine hiring and retention.

Done well, benchmarking gives you:
- Confidence in your offers  
- Consistency in your pay practices  
- A stronger position in a competitive talent market  

If you’re not confident in your current approach, it’s worth taking a closer look. Small errors in benchmarking can lead to big consequences over time.
