# How Small Companies Can Benchmark Salaries Without Expensive Data Tools

If you’re running a small business, figuring out what to pay your team can feel like guesswork.

You want to be competitive—but not reckless. Fair—but still sustainable.

The problem? Most “official” compensation data tools are expensive, overly complex, or built for companies much larger than yours.

The good news: you don’t need them.

With the right approach, you can benchmark salaries confidently using free and low-cost data—and make decisions that attract talent without overpaying.

This guide walks you through exactly how to do it.

---

## 1. Start With the Role—Not the Title

Before you look at any salary data, get clear on what you’re actually benchmarking.

Job titles are inconsistent across companies. A “Marketing Manager” at one company might be doing the work of a “Director” at another.

Instead, focus on:
- Core responsibilities  
- Scope of ownership  
- Years of experience required  
- Team size (if applicable)  
- Decision-making authority  

**Example:**

If your “Operations Manager” is handling vendor contracts, logistics, and internal systems solo, you’re likely benchmarking against a more senior role than the title suggests.

**Tip:** Write a short role summary (3–5 bullet points) before researching salaries. This keeps your comparisons grounded in reality.

---

## 2. Use Multiple Sources of Public Salary Data

You don’t need a $20K compensation platform to get directional accuracy.

Instead, pull data from a mix of publicly available sources:

- Glassdoor  
- Payscale  
- LinkedIn Salary Insights  
- Indeed Salary Tool  
- Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)  

Each source has bias:
- Some skew toward larger companies  
- Some rely on self-reported data  
- Some lag behind current market conditions  

**The key is triangulation.**

Don’t rely on one number—look for patterns across sources.

**What you’re looking for:**
- Consistent ranges (e.g., $65K–$85K showing up repeatedly)  
- Median or midpoint trends  
- Outliers (and whether to ignore them)

---

## 3. Adjust for Location (This Matters More Than You Think)

Salary is heavily influenced by geography—even in remote roles.

If you ignore location, your benchmarks will be off.

### How to adjust:

**Option 1: Use location filters**  
Most salary tools allow you to filter by city or region. Start there.

**Option 2: Apply a simple adjustment factor**  
If you're hiring outside major metro areas:

- Major cities (NYC, SF): baseline (100%)  
- Mid-sized cities: ~85–95%  
- Smaller markets: ~75–90%  

**Example:**

If a role pays $100K in San Francisco, a comparable role in a smaller market might reasonably fall between $75K–$90K.

**For remote teams:**

Decide your philosophy upfront:
- Pay based on employee location  
- Pay based on company HQ  
- Pay a flat national rate  

There’s no “correct” answer—but inconsistency will create problems fast.

---

## 4. Understand Salary Ranges (Not Just Averages)

A common mistake: anchoring on a single number.

Real compensation decisions live in ranges.

A typical salary range includes:
- **Minimum (entry-level for the role)**  
- **Midpoint (fully competent performer)**  
- **Maximum (top-end, highly experienced)**  

**Example:**

$70K – $90K – $110K  

If you hire someone with:
- Less experience → offer near the bottom  
- Solid experience → target the midpoint  
- Exceptional experience → justify higher  

**Important:**  
If you consistently hire above the midpoint, your range is probably wrong.

---

## 5. Sanity Check Against Your Business Reality

Market data is a guide—not a rule.

You still need to align compensation with:
- Your revenue  
- Your margins  
- Your growth stage  

If the “market rate” doesn’t fit your business, you have three options:
1. Adjust the role scope  
2. Hire more junior  
3. Offer non-cash value (flexibility, growth, equity, etc.)

Ignoring this step is how small companies get into trouble—fast.

---

## 6. Document Your Approach (Yes, Even If You're Small)

This is where most small companies drop the ball.

Even a simple internal framework helps you:
- Stay consistent across hires  
- Defend decisions to employees  
- Avoid reactive, emotional salary decisions  

Keep it lightweight:
- Role definitions  
- Data sources used  
- Location approach  
- Salary ranges  

That’s it.

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Using one data source and calling it “market rate”  
- Benchmarking based on job titles alone  
- Ignoring location differences  
- Hiring above range without adjusting the range  
- Making one-off exceptions that become the norm  

---

## The Bottom Line

You don’t need expensive tools to benchmark salaries effectively.

You need:
- Clear role definitions  
- Multiple data sources  
- Thoughtful location adjustments  
- A realistic understanding of ranges  

Do this well, and you’ll make smarter offers, build trust with your team, and avoid costly compensation mistakes.
